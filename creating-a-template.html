Creating a template to make it easier to get design work ready for development

21 April 2023

Getting design work ready for development is often referred to as ‘handing over’ work. This implies that designers and other user-centred design professionals are no longer involved in that work and perpetuates a siloed way of working. I want to work collaboratively with developers during the build phase, not chuck something over the fence and never see it again.

Getting work ready for development can be done in a variety of ways, but whatever you prefer, lacking a consistent approach could mean you risk leaving out important details. If something is missed, your team may not meet its sprint goals or may need to raise a bug to rectify the issue. This will end up causing more work for both the designers and the developers.

Designers should be able to get involved early on in refining tickets and working on acceptance criteria, alongside people such as business analysts, technical architects, product owners and managers.

I’ve recently worked on a template to help me make sure I don’t miss anything in my ready for development documentation:

Description of work

In my experience developers appreciate getting an understanding of why they’re being asked to do a piece of work. To articulate this you can include:

  • user needs
  • the problems you’re trying to solve
  • the intended outcome of doing the work
  • links to any research or design reports that give more context

Design artefacts

What design artefacts to include depends on your prototyping tool of choice and the complexity of the work you’ve been doing, but can include links to:

  • your prototype (if it’s hosted online, be sure to include any usernames or passwords)
  • images of user flows that show the journey
  • videos of a demo of the prototype

Design rules

Accessibility requirements

Make notes of any requirements such as:

  • heading structure and hierarchy (what should be H1, H2 and so on)
  • fieldsets and legends
  • visually hidden text
  • alt text
  • making sure terms are read out properly by screenreaders

Error handling and data catalogue

If it’s applicable to your design, include mock ups of how the design should behave in these scenarios:

  • validation errors (for example a user has inputted an invalid date)
  • empty states (for example a user has searched and got no results)
  • end pages (such as service unavailable, page not found)

If you’ve designed a form, you can provide a data catalogue for input fields, to give detail such as:

  • what characters are accepted
  • character limits

Measuring success

Let developers know if you need any analytics in place in order to measure the success of the implemented design.

User stories

Include any user stories you’ve captured that explain the expected functionality or user needs.

Acceptance criteria

There are other templates you can use but I use the Given/When/Then template to explain how the design should behave and what should happen when users interact with it.

Don’t expect to have everything covered at this stage, as you can develop your acceptance criteria further with team members such as business analysts, developers and product owners.

During the development phase

It’s a good idea to include a design quality assurance stage in your workflow, so designers are included in signing off work.

Using a consistent approach to getting work ready for development should make things easier not just for designers, but the rest of the team too.
